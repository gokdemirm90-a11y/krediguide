# Getting the Best Mortgage Rate in 2026

The mortgage market in 2026 will likely be competitive, which is good news for borrowers willing to shop around. Whether you're buying your first home or refinancing, getting the best rate can save you thousands of dollars over the life of your loan. Here's what you need to know.

Check Your Credit Before You Apply

Your credit score is the biggest factor lenders look at when deciding your rate. Before applying for a mortgage, pull your free credit report and fix any errors. Pay down credit card balances if possible and avoid opening new credit accounts. A score above 740 typically gets the best rates, but even improving from 650 to 700 can lower your rate noticeably and save you real money.

Compare Multiple Lenders

Don't accept the first rate you're offered. Contact at least three to five lenders—banks, credit unions, and online companies—and ask for written quotes. Make sure you're comparing the same loan type and terms. Different lenders quote different rates based on their own costs and business models. This shopping usually takes just a few hours but can reveal rate differences of 0.5 to 1 percent, which adds up to tens of thousands of dollars.

Consider Your Down Payment and Loan Type

A larger down payment typically means a better rate because you're borrowing less money. Putting down 20 percent instead of 10 percent can lower your rate by several points. Also, compare fixed-rate and adjustable-rate mortgages. Fixed rates are more stable and predictable. Adjustable rates start lower but can rise later, which adds risk. For most people, a 30-year fixed mortgage is the safest choice.

Final Thoughts

Getting the best mortgage rate requires some homework, but the effort is worth it. Spend time improving your credit, get multiple quotes from different lenders, and understand your loan options. These steps might save you hundreds of dollars each month and tens of thousands over your loan's lifetime. In 2026's competitive market, prepared borrowers will win the best deals. Start early, ask questions, and don't rush the process.
