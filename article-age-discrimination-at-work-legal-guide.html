# Age Discrimination at Work: Legal Guide

Getting older shouldn't cost you your job. Yet many workers face unfair treatment because of their age. Understanding your rights helps you protect yourself and take action if needed.

What Is Age Discrimination?

Age discrimination happens when an employer treats you worse because of your age. This includes hiring, firing, pay, promotions, or job assignments. Federal law protects workers who are 40 and older. You're protected whether you're too old or too young for a job—the law covers both situations.

Common warning signs include: being passed over for promotions favoring younger workers, comments about your age, being excluded from meetings or projects, or sudden job loss after years of good performance. Sometimes discrimination is obvious. Other times it's hidden behind different reasons like "restructuring" or "we need fresh energy."

Know Your Legal Rights

The Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA) is your main protection. It covers companies with 20+ employees. Many states have stronger laws protecting even more workers.

You can file a complaint with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) for free. You don't need a lawyer to start. The EEOC will investigate your claim. If they find discrimination, they'll try to resolve it. You can also file a lawsuit, and you may recover lost wages, damages, and attorney fees if you win.

Important: There are time limits. You generally have 180-300 days to file with the EEOC, depending on your state. Don't wait too long.

Practical Steps to Protect Yourself

Document everything. Save emails, messages, and notes about age-related comments or unfair treatment. Write down dates, what happened, and who witnessed it.

Keep records of your work. Save performance reviews, accomplishments, and emails showing your strong performance. This proves you're a valuable employee.

Report it internally first. Tell your HR department or manager in writing (email works). Keep copies. Many companies require this before you can sue.

Get help. Contact the EEOC or consult an employment lawyer. Many offer free initial consultations.

Know what you can discuss. You can talk to coworkers about wages and working conditions. This is legally protected.

Final Thoughts

Age discrimination is illegal, and you have options if it happens to you. Don't ignore warning signs—act early. Gather evidence, report problems, and seek guidance from the EEOC or a lawyer. You've built valuable experience and deserve fair treatment at work.
