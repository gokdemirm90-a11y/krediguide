# Interest-Only Mortgage: Pros and Cons

An interest-only mortgage lets you pay just the interest for a set period—usually 5 to 10 years. After that, you start paying both interest and principal. These loans aren't common, but they're worth understanding if you're shopping for a home.

The Big Advantages

Interest-only mortgages have lower monthly payments at first. This can help if you're tight on cash now but expect your income to grow later. Some buyers use this breathing room to handle other debts or build savings.

These mortgages also work well if you're buying an investment property. You might rent it out and use the rental income to cover payments while building equity slowly.

The Major Drawbacks

After the interest-only period ends, your payments jump dramatically. Be prepared for a shock—sometimes payments double or triple. If your income hasn't grown as expected, you might struggle to afford them.

Here's the real problem: you're not building home equity during the interest-only phase. You're just paying the bank. If your home value drops, you could owe more than your house is worth. Plus, these loans typically carry higher interest rates than standard mortgages.

Before You Sign

Take these steps before committing:

Run the numbers. Calculate what your payment will be after the interest-only period ends. Make sure you can actually afford it, not just hope you can.

Check your timeline. Only consider this loan if you're confident your income will increase significantly. If you might move or refinance within a few years, it could work. Otherwise, avoid it.

Read the fine print. Some interest-only loans have strict rules about what happens after the initial period. Some allow you to extend it; others require a full refinance.

Talk to a financial advisor. Get a second opinion before making this decision. A professional can review your specific situation.

The Bottom Line

Interest-only mortgages aren't right for most homebuyers. They work best for people with growing incomes, investment properties, or short timelines. If you're a first-time buyer looking for a primary residence, a standard 30-year mortgage is usually safer. The lower initial payment isn't worth the risk if it strains your budget later.

Take time to explore all your options before deciding.
