30-Year vs 15-Year Mortgage: Which Is Better?

📊 Mortgage · June 2, 2026 · 5 min read
AD
Advertisement

Choosing between a 30-year and 15-year mortgage is one of the biggest financial decisions you'll make as a homebuyer. Both have real advantages — and the right choice depends entirely on your financial situation and goals.

The Core Difference: Payment vs. Total Cost

A 30-year mortgage spreads your payments over 360 months, keeping your monthly payment low. A 15-year mortgage doubles your payoff speed but significantly increases your monthly obligation.

When a 30-Year Makes More Sense

Choose a 30-year mortgage if:

You're a first-time buyer and need the lower payment to qualify. Your income is variable or you want financial flexibility. You plan to invest the monthly difference in the stock market — historically, stock market returns can outpace the interest savings of a 15-year mortgage. You live in a high cost-of-living area where the difference in payments is substantial.

Key insight: A 30-year mortgage doesn't mean you're locked in for 30 years. You can always make extra principal payments to pay it off faster — without the obligation of a higher required monthly payment.

When a 15-Year Makes More Sense

Choose a 15-year mortgage if:

You have stable, high income and can comfortably afford the higher payment. You're closer to retirement and want to eliminate the mortgage before you stop working. You want to build equity faster, especially in a rising market. The interest rate difference is significant enough to make the math compelling.

The Math You Should Actually Do

Don't just compare payments — compare your total financial picture. Take the monthly difference between the two payments. Ask yourself: if I invested that difference every month for 30 years, what would I have? At a 7% average annual return, even $500/month invested becomes over $600,000 in 30 years. That often exceeds the interest savings of the 15-year mortgage.

The bottom line: If you can comfortably afford the 15-year payment without stretching your budget, it's an excellent choice. If you're on the edge, the 30-year gives you breathing room — and discipline in investing the difference can produce similar outcomes.
