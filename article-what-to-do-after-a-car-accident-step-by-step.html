# What Is PMI and How to Avoid It?

If you're buying a home with a down payment less than 20%, you've likely heard the term PMI. Understanding what it is and how to avoid it can save you thousands of dollars over the life of your mortgage.

Understanding PMI Basics

PMI stands for Private Mortgage Insurance. It's an extra fee added to your monthly mortgage payment that protects lenders if you stop paying your loan. Lenders require PMI when you put down less than 20% because they consider the loan riskier. The cost typically runs between 0.5% and 2% of your loan amount annually, split into monthly payments.

Here's the catch: you're paying for insurance that protects the lender, not yourself. This money goes to the insurance company, not toward building equity in your home.

How to Avoid PMI Before Buying

The most straightforward way to skip PMI is saving a 20% down payment. If you're buying a $300,000 home, that means $60,000 down. This takes time, but it's worth the wait.

If waiting isn't realistic, consider these alternatives:

Gift funds from family – You can use money gifted by relatives as part of your down payment, reducing the amount you need to borrow.

Piggyback loans – Some buyers take out two mortgages simultaneously: one for 80% of the home value and another for 10-15%. This covers the gap without PMI, though you'll pay higher interest rates on the second loan.

First-time buyer programs – Many states and local governments offer down payment assistance programs with relaxed PMI requirements.

Getting Rid of PMI After You Buy

If you already have PMI, you're not stuck with it forever. Once your home value increases or you've paid down your mortgage to 80% of the original purchase price, you can request PMI removal.

You can speed this up by making extra principal payments toward your mortgage. Each dollar paid toward principal gets you closer to that 20% equity mark.

Some lenders automatically cancel PMI once you hit 22% equity, so check your loan documents.

The Bottom Line

PMI adds real money to your mortgage costs, but it's not permanent. Whether you avoid it upfront through a larger down payment or eliminate it through extra payments, reducing PMI should be part of your homeownership strategy. Run the numbers with your lender to find the approach that makes the most sense for your situation.
