# How to Buy a Car with Bad Credit

Having a low credit score doesn't mean you can't buy a car. While it's harder and more expensive, thousands of Americans get car loans with bad credit every year. Here's how to make it happen.

Get Your Credit Report First

Before you start shopping, pull your credit report for free at annualcreditreport.com. Look for mistakes—lenders sometimes report wrong information that hurts your score. If you spot errors, dispute them right away. You should also know your actual credit score so you understand what lenders will see.

Even small improvements matter. Pay down credit card balances if you can, and make sure all your bills are current. These quick wins might help you qualify for better loan terms.

Shop Around for the Right Lender

Traditional banks are tough on bad credit borrowers. Instead, try credit unions, which often have looser requirements. Online lenders and buy-here-pay-here dealerships (where the dealer finances the car) also work with bad credit buyers. Compare at least three lenders before choosing one.

Be prepared to pay higher interest rates. With bad credit, you might pay 10-20% interest instead of 4-6%. That's expensive, but it's the cost of borrowing with risk. Calculate what you can actually afford each month before committing.

Make Your Application Stronger

A larger down payment is your best tool. Put down 20% or more if possible—this shows lenders you're serious and reduces their risk. If you can't afford that, save up as much as you can.

Find a co-signer if you have someone willing to take responsibility if you don't pay. This dramatically improves your chances of approval.

Finally, aim for an affordable car. Don't reach for an expensive vehicle. A reliable used Honda or Toyota that costs $5,000-$10,000 is smarter than a $20,000 car you'll struggle to pay for.

The Bottom Line

Buying a car with bad credit requires patience and planning, but it's absolutely doable. Work on your credit report, shop around for honest lenders, and make a down payment. You'll pay more than someone with good credit, but you'll get wheels—and improving your payment history helps rebuild your credit for the future.
