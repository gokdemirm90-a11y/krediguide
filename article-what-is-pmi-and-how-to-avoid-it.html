# What Happens If You Miss a Mortgage Payment?

Missing a mortgage payment can feel scary, but understanding what happens next helps you take action. The good news is that lenders typically don't foreclose immediately. Here's what you need to know.

The First 30 Days: Grace Period and Late Fees

Most lenders give you a grace period—usually 15 days after your payment due date. During this time, you can pay without penalty. After that, you'll face a late fee, typically 3-6% of your monthly payment. Your lender will likely send you a reminder notice. This is important: even during the grace period, you're technically late, and it may show up on your credit report.

30-90 Days: Serious Consequences Begin

Once you're 30 days late, your lender will report this to credit bureaus. Your credit score drops—sometimes by 100 points or more. You'll receive official notices and phone calls from your lender. At 90 days overdue, your loan enters "default" status. This is when lenders get serious about collection efforts. Interest and fees keep adding up. However, you still have time to fix this before foreclosure happens.

120+ Days: Foreclosure Risk

After 120 days of missed payments, lenders can begin foreclosure proceedings. This varies by state, but the process typically takes several months. The lender takes back the property and sells it to recover the money owed. Foreclosure devastates your credit for years and can make it hard to rent, buy again, or get loans.

Practical Tips to Protect Yourself

Act immediately. Contact your lender right away if you can't pay. Many offer hardship programs, payment plans, or loan modifications.

Explore forbearance. This temporarily reduces or pauses payments. You repay the missed amount later.

Ask about refinancing. If you have decent credit, refinancing might lower payments.

Seek counseling. HUD-approved housing counselors offer free advice at housing.gov.

Consider a loan modification. Your lender might agree to change terms to make payments manageable.

Conclusion

Missing one payment isn't the end of the road. Most lenders prefer working with you over foreclosure. The key is communicating early and exploring options before you fall too far behind. Your home is your biggest investment—protecting it starts with taking action today.
