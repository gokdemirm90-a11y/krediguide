# How to File a Wage Theft Claim

Wage theft happens when employers don't pay workers the money they've earned. This might include unpaid overtime, minimum wage violations, or withheld paychecks. If you believe your employer owes you money, you have options to recover it.

Document Everything First

Before filing a claim, gather all your evidence. Keep copies of your paychecks, pay stubs, and any written communication with your boss about pay. Write down dates, hours worked, and what you were promised versus what you received. Take screenshots of emails or texts that mention your pay. Store these documents in a safe place—cloud storage works well. The stronger your documentation, the better your case looks.

Know Your Filing Options

You have several paths forward. First, try talking directly with your employer's HR department or payroll office. Sometimes wage theft is a simple mistake that gets fixed quickly. If that doesn't work, you can file a complaint with your state's Department of Labor. Most states have online complaint forms on their websites, and the process is usually free. You can also hire an employment lawyer or contact your state's legal aid office if you can't afford one. Some lawyers work on contingency, meaning they only get paid if you win.

Take Action at the Right Agency

Contact your state's Department of Labor—not the federal Department of Labor. Each state handles wage theft differently and has its own rules. Your state agency will investigate your claim at no cost to you. Be prepared to answer questions about your job duties, how you were paid, and how long the wage theft occurred. Provide copies of your documentation. The investigation process typically takes several weeks or months.

Conclusion

Wage theft is illegal, and you deserve to be paid for your work. Start by documenting everything and contacting your state's Department of Labor. Don't worry about retaliation—it's illegal for employers to punish workers for filing wage theft claims. If you need help navigating the process, reach out to legal aid organizations in your state. You have rights, and there are people ready to help you fight for the money you've earned.
