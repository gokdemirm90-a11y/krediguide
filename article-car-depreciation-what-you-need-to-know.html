# Bridge Loan: What It Is and When to Use It

A bridge loan is short-term money you borrow to cover expenses while waiting for something else to happen—usually selling your old home or getting permanent financing. Think of it as a financial bridge that helps you cross a gap between two transactions.

What a Bridge Loan Does

When you're buying a new house but haven't sold your current one yet, you face a timing problem. You need money now, but your old home's sale funds won't arrive for weeks or months. A bridge loan lets you borrow against your current home's equity to make your new purchase without waiting.

The lender essentially advances you money based on what your house is worth. You repay the bridge loan once your original home sells or your primary mortgage closes. These loans typically last three to twelve months.

When Bridge Loans Make Sense

Bridge loans work best in competitive real estate markets where you need to act quickly. If you've found your dream home but still own another property, a bridge loan shows sellers you're serious and can close fast.

They also help when you need to move immediately for a job but can't wait for your home to sell. Bridge loans solve timing mismatches between buying and selling.

However, they're expensive. Bridge loans charge higher interest rates than regular mortgages—sometimes two to three percent more. You're also paying fees and possibly two mortgage payments temporarily.

Practical Tips for Borrowers

Before considering a bridge loan, get your current home appraised to know your equity. Speak with your bank or credit union first—they often offer better rates than specialized lenders.

Compare costs carefully. Calculate all fees and interest charges for the expected loan period. Sometimes it's cheaper to negotiate a later closing date on your new house instead.

Check your credit score beforehand. Better credit scores mean better rates. Also, make sure you can afford temporary double mortgage payments if your home takes longer to sell.

Finally, include a contingency plan. Ask your lender what happens if your old home doesn't sell quickly. Some bridge loans have built-in protections.

The Bottom Line

Bridge loans solve real problems for homebuyers facing timing issues. But they're expensive and work best as a last resort when you absolutely need funds immediately. Explore other options first, compare all costs, and only use a bridge loan if the math makes sense for your situation.
